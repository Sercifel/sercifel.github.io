2027 Investment Forward: Key Upgrade Trends & Budget Advice for Can-Making Equipment

Feb 12, 2026

Explore the critical 2027 shifts in metal packaging, from autonomous AI-driven production lines to carbon-neutral mandates. Learn why the 12-18 month lead time makes 2026 the vital window for strategic procurement and ROI optimization.

Is Your 2027 Production Capacity Already at Risk?

Imagine it is January 2027. Your biggest competitor has just launched a new carbon-neutral beverage line using ultra-lightweight cans produced on a fully automated, low-energy assembly line. Meanwhile, your facility is struggling with rising energy costs, frequent downtime on 15-year-old machinery, and a shrinking pool of skilled technicians who can operate manual equipment.

For many in the metal packaging industry, this isn't a distant "what if"—it is a looming reality. The current challenge isn't just about what to buy, but when to buy it. With global supply chains for high-precision components still recovering and a surge in demand for sustainable packaging, the lead time for high-end can-making machinery has stretched significantly. If you aren't planning your 2027 budget and securing your delivery slots now, you may find yourself at the back of a very long, very expensive line.

Trend 1: The Transition from "Smart" to "Autonomous" Lines

The Claim: AI is shifting from a monitoring tool to an active operator.

In 2027, the most successful can-making facilities will utilize equipment that possesses "self-healing" capabilities, where the machinery uses edge computing to analyze vibrations, heat, and output quality to make micro-adjustments without human intervention.

The Context: Why this change is happening now.

The industry faces a dual pressure: a global shortage of specialized mechanical engineers and an uncompromising demand for higher output speeds. Traditional PLC (Programmable Logic Controller) systems can maintain a set speed, but they cannot "learn" from environmental variables like humidity or slight variations in metal coil thickness. AI-integrated systems bridge this gap by processing millions of data points per second.

The Evidence: Impact on the Bottom Line.

According to a recent report by Mordor Intelligence (2024) on the Metal Packaging Market, the integration of IoT and AI in production lines is expected to drive a Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of 4.5% through 2029. Companies that have implemented early-stage AI diagnostics have reported a 12% increase in Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE). Source: Mordor Intelligence - Metal Packaging Market Analysis

Trend 2: The "Green" Mandate – Decarbonizing the Production Floor

The Claim: Equipment energy efficiency will become a primary cost-saving metric, surpassing initial purchase price.

By 2027, the "cost of ownership" will be redefined by carbon taxes and energy consumption. Upgrading to equipment that utilizes induction curing instead of traditional gas-fired ovens is no longer an environmental choice—it is a fiscal necessity.

The Context: The regulatory landscape is tightening.

International frameworks, such as the EU's Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR), are setting strict targets for recyclability and carbon footprints. As these regulations influence global supply chains, even manufacturers outside the EU must adapt to remain as viable suppliers for multinational brands like Coca-Cola or AB InBev.

The Evidence: Energy reduction statistics.

Research from Smithers indicates that the shift toward "Dry" forming and advanced coating technologies can reduce the energy intensity of aluminum can production by up to 25%. Modern 2-piece can lines are now being engineered to operate with zero-liquid discharge (ZLD) systems to meet these 2027 sustainability benchmarks. Source: Smithers - The Future of Metal Packaging to 2027

The Hot Topic: The AI Controversy in Can-Making

One of the most debated topics in the industry today is the "Black Box" nature of AI in manufacturing. Some veterans argue that relying on algorithms for quality control removes the "human intuition" that has governed can-making for decades. However, the controversy is shifting. The real debate for 2027 isn't whether AI should be used, but who owns the data.

Manufacturers are now demanding "Open AI Architecture" where the machine data stays with the factory, rather than being sent back to the equipment OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer). This ensures competitive secrets regarding proprietary forming processes remain secure while still reaping the benefits of machine learning.

Industry FAQ: Addressing Procurement Concerns

Q1: We still have five years of life left in our current line. Why should we look at 2027 upgrades now? A: The "technological cliff" is approaching. By 2027, the cost of spare parts for non-digital machines will skyrocket as OEMs pivot to support new platforms. Furthermore, the 12-18 month lead time means that an inquiry made in late 2025 results in a 2027 operational date. Waiting until 2027 to start the process means you won't be upgraded until 2029.

Q2: How do we justify the cost of "Autonomous" features to our board? A: Focus on the "Cost of Quality." Calculate your current annual loss due to micro-stops, scrap metal, and manual inspection errors. In most high-speed lines, an AI-driven system pays for itself within 18-24 months purely through waste reduction and increased throughput.

Q3: Is 2-piece or 3-piece equipment a better investment for 2027? A: This depends on your market. While 2-piece cans dominate the beverage sector due to speed and weight, 3-piece cans are seeing a resurgence in the premium food and industrial sectors because of the flexibility in heights and the ability to use different materials for the body and ends. However, both must now feature digital tracking and low-energy welding/seaming technology.

The Procurement Landscape: Leading Global Manufacturers

When planning your 2027 upgrades, it is essential to look at a mix of established Western giants and agile, high-tech Asian manufacturers. Here are 11 key players shaping the future of can-making machinery:

  1. Stolle Machinery (USA): A global leader in high-speed can-making equipment, known for their complete line solutions and "E-Series" energy-efficient modules.
  2. Belvac (USA): Specializes in shaping and necking technology, focusing heavily on lightweighting capabilities for the beverage industry.
  3. Crown Holdings (USA/Global): While a major producer, their machinery division sets the standard for high-volume efficiency and sustainable design.
  4. Ball Corporation (USA): A pioneer in the "Circular Economy," focusing on aluminum packaging solutions that integrate seamlessly with closed-loop recycling systems.
  5. CarnaudMetalbox Engineering (UK): Famous for their seamer technology and recent pushes into "smart" machinery with integrated sensors.
  6. Hinterkopf (Germany): A leader in digital printing for cans and impact extrusion, perfect for brands looking for high-quality aesthetics without traditional labels.
  7. Mall + Herlan (Germany): Specialists in metal packaging for aerosols and tubes, focusing on high-precision mechanical engineering.
  8. SLAC Precision Equipment (China): A rapidly growing provider of high-speed easy-open end production lines and digital inspection systems.
  9. Soudronic (Switzerland): The gold standard for 3-piece can welding technology, consistently innovating in energy-efficient welding processes.
  10. Metlac (Italy): Focused on the coatings and lacquers that are essential for the next generation of BPA-free, eco-friendly cans.
