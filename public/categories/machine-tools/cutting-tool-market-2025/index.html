Cutting Tool Market: 2025 Recovery and Tariff Headwinds

Nov 06, 2025

Analyze the 2025 U.S. cutting tool market recovery. Explore how 50% tariffs on steel/aluminum derivatives and aerospace resilience are shaping the 2026 manufacturing outlook.

The Cutting Tool Market Report (CTMR), a specialized collaboration between AMT – The Association For Manufacturing Technology and the U.S. Cutting Tool Institute (USCTI), provides critical monthly data on U.S. manufacturers’ consumption of cutting tools. Since the cutting tool is considered the primary consumable in the manufacturing process, CTMR analysis is a true measure of actual production levels and serves as a leading indicator for industrial economic shifts.

U.S. Cutting Tool Market Dynamics: Recovery Trends and Tariff Headwinds in 2025

I. Analysis of Latest Data: September 2025 Performance

The September 2025 data revealed a strong upturn in the cutting tool market, providing a positive close to the third quarter amidst lingering concerns over costs and geopolitical stability.

September 2025 Key Shipment Metrics:

• Total Shipments: Cutting tool orders totaled $222 million.

• Month-over-Month (MoM) Growth: Orders increased 5.3% from August 2025.

• Year-over-Year (YoY) Growth: Shipments increased substantially by 14.7% compared to September 2024.

• Year-to-Date (YTD) Status: Despite the robust monthly growth, year-to-date shipments through September 2025 totaled $1.88 billion, which remained 1.1% lower than the same period in 2024.

While the September figures, coupled with positive industrial production trends, suggest that "unstable business conditions may be improving," this optimism is "tempered by the higher cost of products and lower profitability". Unit prices for most cutting tools have seen double-digit increases resulting from hikes caused by tariffs and rising raw material costs.

B. Key Trends and Commentary

The data reveals a clear pattern of declining annual performance stabilizing and reversing in Q3:

• Initial Slump: Shipments showed significant year-over-year declines early in 2025, with February shipments dropping 6.7% YTD compared to 2024. The overall sentiment was that the "calendar year has not kicked off well," due to the "tariff war [denting] the confidence of manufacturing businesses".

• Q2 Stagnation: The market failed to gain traction in Q2. As of May, YTD shipments were down 5.1% compared to 2024. Steve Boyer, president of USCTI, attributed this lag to uncertain tariff negotiations that were "stagnating key market segments" and delaying the anticipated uptick.

• Q3 Resurgence: July and September demonstrated strong MoM and YoY growth. Jack Burley, chairman of AMT’s Cutting Tool Product Group, noted in July that shops still had orders to fill and were "not indicating any signs of slowing down".

III. Market Analysis: Challenges, End Markets, and Outlook

Market performance in 2025 was fundamentally shaped by persistent geopolitical uncertainty and varied strength across industrial sectors.

A. Economic Headwinds and Tariff Fatigue

The manufacturing technology sector faced continuous strain related to complex trade policies:

• Cost Escalation: Tooling manufacturers faced dual cost pressures: increased costs for foreign-sourced products and rising costs for essential raw materials like tungsten carbide. These cost increases are directly hitting manufacturers' operating margins.

• Policy Uncertainty: Tariffs are hindering confidence and investment. The Commerce Department escalated pressure in Q3 2025 by adding 407 product categories of steel and aluminum derivatives to items subject to 50% tariffs under Section 232, which also impacts finished machining centers. AMT reported that this escalation led to a "renewed sense of strain, strategic hesitation, and export headwinds".

• Delayed Investments: The lack of clarity surrounding tariffs means that gaining traction in the second half of 2025 is significantly impacted by the speed at which clarity is gained and manufacturing can be ramped up.

B. Divergence in End Market Demand

End-market demand, traditionally driven by aerospace, automotive, and heavy equipment, began to diverge, with aerospace carrying the momentum.

• Aerospace Sector Resilience: The aerospace market, a heavy user of cutting tool products, remains a steady source of demand. Cutting tools are essential for the sector, which requires high-precision surface finish, demanding machining processes for components like turbine blades. India's defense spending further contributes to international demand in this space.

• Transportation Sector Vulnerability: The transportation sectors of manufacturing—including automotive—are expressing widespread concern regarding backlog, future orders, and inventory. The automotive sector has been lagging due to uncertainty regarding raw materials, inventories, and component acquisition costs. Furthermore, the auto sector faces slowing production due to supply chain issues related to trade and the impact of inflation on consumers.

• Heavy Industry Weakness: The outlook for other heavy industries is mixed or deteriorating:     ◦ The heavy equipment market appears relatively flat.     ◦ Sectors such as construction and agricultural companies are struggling due to tariffs hitting hardest and causing investment delays.     ◦ The agricultural sector outlook "continues to deteriorate" due to factors like record yields, rising carryovers, and declining prices, which have led to layoffs.

C. Outlook for Q4 2025 and Beyond

While the July and September data provided relief, industry leadership remains cautious regarding immediate growth.

• Jack Burley, chairman of AMT’s Cutting Tool Product Group, stated that he does not expect to see any improvements in the fourth quarter of 2025.

• Eli Lustgarten, president of ESL Consultants, suggested that "the worst is over for the cutting tool sector" and that the outlook appears relatively flat, but with a positive bias extending into 2026.

• The broader U.S. Manufacturing Technology Orders (USMTO), which tracks machinery—an investment often preceding cutting tool consumption—showed strong YTD increases through September 2025, up 17.3% compared to 2024. This suggests a solid capital investment pipeline that could eventually fuel cutting tool demand, aligning with the positive bias for 2026.
