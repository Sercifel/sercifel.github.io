How to Produce Food-Grade rPET Sheets from Recycled PET Bottles

Apr 20, 2026

Recycled PET (rPET) has emerged as one of the most commercially viable materials in the sustainable packaging revolution. But producing rPET that meets food-contact standards — what the industry calls food-grade rPET — demands far more than simply melting down old bottles. It requires tightly controlled processing, certified decontamination, and purpose-built equipment designed to handle the unique challenges of post-consumer material.

This guide walks through the complete production process, the technologies that make it work, and the challenges manufacturers must overcome to consistently deliver food-grade rPET sheets suitable for thermoforming into trays, clamshells, and other food packaging.

Why Food-Grade rPET Is in High Demand

The global appetite for food-grade rPET sheet is being driven by two converging forces: regulatory pressure and fundamental shifts in how consumer brands approach packaging.

ESG and Legislative Push

Governments on both sides of the Atlantic have moved from voluntary commitments to binding mandates. The EU's Single-Use Plastics Directive now requires PET beverage bottles to contain at least 25% recycled content by 2025, rising to 30% by 2030. In the United States, several states have enacted extended producer responsibility (EPR) legislation that incentivizes — or effectively mandates — recycled content in packaging. For corporate buyers, ESG reporting frameworks make recycled content a quantifiable metric that directly affects supplier selection and investor relations.

The Food Packaging Transformation

Thermoformed food trays, deli containers, and fresh-produce clamshells represent one of the largest single uses of virgin PET sheet globally. As major retailers and food brands commit to 100% recyclable or recycled packaging portfolios, converting these high-volume applications to rPET has become a strategic priority — not just a sustainability gesture. Food-grade rPET sheet sits at the intersection of supply chain reliability, regulatory compliance, and brand positioning, making it one of the most investable segments in the broader plastics recycling economy.

What Makes rPET "Food-Grade"?

The term "food-grade" is not a marketing claim — it is a technical and regulatory designation that must be earned through demonstrable process control. Three criteria define it.

Decontamination

Post-consumer PET bottles have been in contact with dozens of potential contaminants: pesticide residues, cleaning agents, fuel traces from transportation, printing inks, adhesive labels, and residues from the original beverage itself. Before rPET can legally touch food again, these contaminants must be reduced to levels that pose no migration risk to consumers.

The industry benchmark is the "challenge test" methodology developed by the FDA and endorsed by EFSA, in which surrogates of different molecular sizes and polarities are intentionally introduced into flake and then tracked through the entire extrusion process. A decontamination process is validated only when surrogate concentrations in the final sheet fall below accepted thresholds — typically on the order of parts per billion.

Effective decontamination requires adequate temperature and residence time in the melt phase, combined with vacuum degassing that actively draws volatile contaminants out of the melt stream before the material is formed into sheet.

Intrinsic Viscosity (IV) Control

IV is the single most important indicator of molecular chain length in PET — and by extension, its mechanical performance. Virgin food-packaging PET typically starts at 0.76–0.82 dL/g. Thermal and hydrolytic degradation during reprocessing cause chain scission, reducing IV and with it the impact resistance, clarity, and thermoformability of the final sheet.

Food-grade specifications typically require finished sheet IV above 0.72–0.75 dL/g. Achieving this consistently from post-consumer flake — which arrives with variable IV and unpredictable contamination loads — requires precise moisture control before and during extrusion, careful melt temperature management, and in some cases IV-upgrading through solid-state polycondensation (SSP) upstream or reactive extrusion additives in-line.

FDA / EFSA Compliance

In the United States, a Letter of No Objection (LNO) from the FDA is the standard pathway for food-contact approval of rPET processes. EFSA in Europe evaluates recycling technologies under its own framework and issues opinions on specific approved processes. Both agencies require documented evidence of the decontamination challenge test, process parameter ranges, and quality controls — which means that food-grade production is inseparable from process documentation and traceability.

Bottle-to-Sheet Production Process Explained

Converting baled post-consumer PET bottles into certified food-grade sheet involves a sequence of tightly interdependent steps, each of which can compromise the final product if not controlled correctly.

Step 1 — Sorting and Bale Breaking

Incoming bales are broken, sorted to remove non-PET materials (HDPE caps, PP labels, aluminum cans, glass), and color-separated. Near-infrared (NIR) sorting is standard. Green and colored PET is typically diverted from food-grade streams, which require predominantly clear or lightly tinted flake to meet optical standards.

Step 2 — Washing and Decontamination

Sorted bottles are granulated into flake, then passed through a multi-stage wet washing system — typically hot caustic wash (70–85°C) followed by friction washing, float-sink separation to remove residual caps and labels by density, and rinse stages. The goal is to arrive at clean, label-free, cap-free flake with surface contamination minimized.

Step 3 — Drying

This is one of the most critical control points in the entire process. PET is hygroscopic and readily absorbs atmospheric moisture. If flake enters the extruder with moisture above approximately 50–100 ppm, hydrolytic degradation in the melt will cleave polymer chains, permanently reducing IV. Crystallization dryers operating at 150–180°C with dry air are standard, with dew point monitoring to confirm outlet moisture before flake is conveyed to the extruder.

Step 4 — Extrusion

Dried flake is fed into a single-screw or twin-screw extruder where it is melted, homogenized, and passed through a vacuum degassing zone that draws out residual volatiles, moisture, and low-molecular-weight contaminants. Melt temperature is controlled precisely — typically 260–285°C — to balance adequate flow against thermal degradation. A gear pump downstream of the screw provides pulsation-free, pressure-stable melt delivery to the die.

Step 5 — Filtration

The melt passes through a continuous or discontinuous screen changer with fine filtration media (typically 40–120 micron) to remove undissolved contaminants, carbonized particles, and any remaining inorganic debris. Filtration fineness and pressure drop management are critical to both product quality and line uptime.

Step 6 — Sheet Die and Calendering / Polishing Stack

The filtered melt is distributed through a wide flat die and then passed through a three-roll polishing stack (calendering unit) where thickness is set, surface finish is applied, and the sheet is rapidly quenched to lock in optical clarity and amorphous structure. Roll temperatures, nip pressure, and line speed must be precisely coordinated to maintain gauge uniformity and surface quality.

Step 7 — Haul-off, Trimming, and Winding

The cooled sheet passes through edge trim stations, thickness gauges, and is either wound into rolls or cut into flat sheets depending on customer requirements. Edge trim is typically reintroduced into the process as regrind.

Key Technologies for Stable rPET Sheet Production

Producing food-grade sheet at commercial scale requires more than a standard extrusion line configured for virgin material. Several enabling technologies are non-negotiable for consistent results.

Vacuum Degassing

Post-consumer PET flake contains volatile contaminants that virgin PET does not. A single-stage vent is often insufficient. High-performance lines use double-stage vacuum degassing — sometimes combined with atmospheric venting zones — to maximize the removal of water vapor, low-boiling-point organics, and decontamination byproducts. The depth and efficiency of vacuum extraction directly determines the cleanliness of the melt and, by extension, whether the process can pass a regulatory challenge test.

Melt Filtration

Continuous-screen-changer technology allows the filtration medium to be advanced without interrupting production, maintaining consistent back-pressure and avoiding the quality disruptions that occur every time a screen must be manually changed on a non-continuous system. For food-grade production with high contamination loads in the input flake, continuous filtration is effectively a requirement, not an option.

Automatic Thickness Control (ATC)

Thermoforming customers demand sheet thickness tolerances of ±5% or tighter across the full web width, and even tighter in demanding applications. Achieving this consistently requires closed-loop thickness control: a traversing or full-width gauge (beta-ray, X-ray, or infrared) continuously measures the sheet profile and feeds back to motorized die lip adjusters or haul-off speed controls to correct deviations in real time. Without ATC, gauge variability leads to rejected rolls and — worse — inconsistent wall thickness in formed containers that can fail drop tests.

Melt Temperature and Pressure Stability

Gear pumps decouple the extruder screw from die pressure, providing pulsation-free melt delivery. Static mixers homogenize temperature gradients across the melt cross-section. Together these elements prevent the flow instabilities that manifest as surface defects, optical haze, or sheet gauge variation — particularly important when processing blended flake of variable IV and color.

Common Challenges and How to Solve Them

Even well-designed lines running high-quality flake encounter recurring production challenges. Understanding their root causes is the first step to solving them.

IV Reduction

Symptom: Finished sheet IV below specification; brittle behavior during thermoforming; reduced impact resistance.

Root causes: Insufficient pre-drying (moisture-induced hydrolysis); excessive melt temperature; excessive residence time in the melt phase; high shear in the screw.

Solutions: Verify dew point at dryer outlet before every production run; audit melt temperature settings and minimize the gap between processing temperature and minimum required for adequate flow; optimize screw speed and throughput rate to minimize residence time; consider adding chain extenders or reactive processing additives for feedstocks with chronically low IV; evaluate upstream SSP for severe cases.

Bubbles, Voids, and Black Specks

Symptom: Visible bubbles or cloudiness in the sheet (gas entrapment); black specks distributed through the sheet (carbonization).

Root causes for bubbles: Inadequate drying; vacuum degassing insufficient for contamination level; sudden pressure drops at die exit.

Root causes for black specks: Carbonized material accumulating in dead zones of the flow path (screw design, adapters, die body); inadequate purging between grades; slow-moving material at screen changer during screen changes; degraded regrind being reintroduced.

Solutions: Increase drying time or temperature; improve vacuum level in degassing zones; audit and eliminate dead zones in the flow path; establish rigorous purging protocols; use a continuous screen changer; limit regrind content and ensure regrind is freshly generated, not stored.

Non-Uniform Sheet Thickness

Symptom: Sheet gauge varies across the width (edge-heavy, center-heavy, or irregular profile); rolls have visible camber when slit.

Root causes: Uneven die lip gap; non-uniform melt temperature distribution across the die width; misaligned rolls in the calendering stack; worn or damaged die lips; feedback loop latency in ATC system.

Solutions: Map the die temperature profile and verify heater and thermocouple functionality; manually audit die lip gap across its full width; check roll parallelism and nip pressure distribution; tune ATC control parameters (PID gains) for the specific line speed and material; inspect die lips for wear and replace if polishing cannot restore flatness.
