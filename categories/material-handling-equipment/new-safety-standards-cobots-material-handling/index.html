New Safety Standards for Collaborative Robots (Cobots) in Material Handling

Mar 18, 2026

Summarizes 2025 updates to ISO 10218 and ANSI/A3 R15.06 for safer collaborative robot use in material handling.

The landscape of industrial automation has been significantly transformed with the advent of collaborative robots, or cobots. These machines have revolutionized manufacturing and material handling by working alongside human operators, enhancing productivity and flexibility. However, as their integration into various industries increases, so does the need for stringent safety standards to ensure that human-robot collaboration remains safe and efficient. The recent updates to ISO 10218:2025 and ANSI/A3 R15.06-2025 standards mark a pivotal shift in how safety is approached in collaborative robot applications, particularly in material handling.

The Evolution of Safety Standards

ISO 10218:2025

ISO 10218:2025 represents a significant update from its previous version in 2011. The most notable change is the full incorporation of collaborative robot safety into the core standard, which was previously addressed separately under ISO/TS 15066. This integration reflects the technological advancements and growing use of cobots, dictating a critical change in how risk assessment and compliance are approached.

ISO 10218-1:2025 focuses on the safety of the robot itself, detailing the safety functions that manufacturers must provide in both hardware and software. In contrast, ISO 10218-2:2025 addresses the integration of the robot into the system and application, including the design of work zones and the classification of safety functions as required, optional, or conditional.

ANSI/A3 R15.06-2025

In the United States, the ANSI/A3 R15.06-2025 standard has been updated to harmonize with ISO 10218:2025. This update represents the most significant safety revision in U.S. robotics in over a decade, bringing clarity to safety requirements and introducing considerations like cybersecurity. The standard emphasizes collaborative applications rather than treating collaborative robots as a distinct type, allowing for more nuanced safety controls in human-robot interaction environments.

Key Changes in Safety Standards

New Classification System

The revised ISO 10218:2025 introduces a new classification system for robots, including those used for collaboration. This system dictates the required functional safety validation rigor, with many applications previously marketed as cobots now falling under stricter categories like Class I, requiring additional safety measures.

Cybersecurity Considerations

Reflecting modern interconnectedness, the revised standards include new cybersecurity requirements to protect safety functions from cyber threats. This ensures that a compromised network cannot inadvertently disable safety measures, which is crucial in maintaining the integrity of collaborative applications.

Enhanced Guidelines for Specific Tasks

The standards now provide clearer safety guidance for end-of-arm tooling (EOAT) and manual load/unload procedures. These guidelines are essential for ensuring that cobots can safely perform specific tasks without posing risks to human operators.

Implications for Material Handling

Risk Assessment and Compliance

The updated standards necessitate a thorough, application-specific risk assessment for all collaborative applications. This approach ensures that all hazards are identified and reduced to acceptable levels, aligning with the gold standard for compliance. For material handling, this means evaluating factors such as human intention and behavior, workspace layout, and task-specific hazards.

Safety Features in Cobots

Collaborative robots are equipped with safety features such as power and force limiting, emergency stop buttons, and speed reduction when humans approach. Many models also use safety-rated controllers, protective covers, and 3D vision or sensors to track operator movement. These features are crucial in material handling applications, where the risk of collision and injury is high.

Training and Support

Proper training and support are essential for the safe implementation of cobots in material handling. Employees must be thoroughly trained on the unique abilities and tasks of cobots, with detailed instruction periods to ensure they can operate safely alongside these machines.

Conclusion

The 2025 revisions to ISO 10218 and ANSI/A3 R15.06 standards mark a significant step towards a safer, more flexible human-robot workspace. By embracing the shift to collaborative applications and prioritizing compliance, industries can ensure that as automation advances, worker safety remains paramount. For material handling, these updates provide a robust framework for integrating cobots into operations, enhancing productivity while minimizing risks.
