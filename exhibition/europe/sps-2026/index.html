SPS 2026: What to Expect at Nuremberg's Annual Automation Benchmark

Nov 24, 2026

Every November, a significant portion of the global industrial automation community converges on Nuremberg. For engineers, engineering managers, procurement specialists, and technology strategists working anywhere along the production automation chain, SPS has become the calendar fixture that sets the tone for the year ahead. The 2026 edition, scheduled for 24 to 26 November at NürnbergMesse, marks the 35th time the fair has taken place, and the momentum heading into it is measurably stronger than it was a year ago.

Why Nuremberg Holds This Position

It is worth asking why a city of under 600,000 people in southern Germany has become the effective heartbeat of the European industrial automation sector. The answer is partly historical and partly practical. Bavaria's dense network of mechanical engineering firms, electrical component manufacturers, and mid-size automation integrators created a natural gravitational pull decades ago. NürnbergMesse itself is purpose-built for technical trade fairs, with 15 interconnected halls that allow the kind of structured, segment-by-segment exhibition layout that an industry as technically differentiated as automation actually needs.

SPS, formerly known as SPS IPC Drives, is held at the Nuremberg Exhibition Center and is one of the most significant international trade fairs in the domain of smart and digital automation solutions. One of its structural advantages over competing events is the emphasis on complete system displays rather than isolated components. A visitor evaluating conveyor control architecture, for example, can walk from a sensor manufacturer to a drive systems specialist to a software-defined PLC vendor within the same exhibition zone, rather than piecing together a market picture across different events in different countries.

What SPS 2025 Confirmed

The 2025 edition provided some of the clearest signals yet about where the industry is heading. For three days from 25 to 27 November 2025, around 56,000 visitors informed themselves about product innovations and solutions at 1,175 exhibitors across 15 exhibition halls covering a total area of 122,000 sqm. That visitor count represents a meaningful step up from 2024, when SPS 2024 was attended by around 51,300 visitors. A roughly 9 percent year-on-year increase is not incidental in an environment where German manufacturing is facing real cost pressures and macroeconomic headwinds.

In discussions with exhibitors, attendees addressed their most pressing challenges: process automation, cost reductions for German machinery manufacturers to remain internationally competitive, supply-chain resilience, open systems, and compliance with new regulations such as the Machinery Regulation, the Cyber Resilience Act, and the NIS-2 Directive, as well as energy efficiency. This tells you something about who attends SPS and what they are there to do. These are not passive spectators browsing product brochures. They are technical decision-makers with specific implementation challenges, sent by their companies to evaluate suppliers and validate technology choices against real operating requirements.

The Technology Agenda Shaping the 2026 Edition

The dominant thread at SPS 2025 was industrial AI, and there is no indication it will recede in 2026. One of the central themes at SPS 2025 was the increasing integration of artificial intelligence into industrial processes. Exhibitors showcased concrete applications, from AI-supported quality inspection to adaptive control systems and intelligent maintenance solutions based on real-time data. all-about-industries

What distinguished the 2025 conversation from earlier years was the shift from concept to deployment. Exhibitors were not showing proof-of-concept demos for what AI might eventually do on the factory floor. Many were presenting implementations already running in production environments, with quantifiable outcomes in predictive maintenance cycles and defect detection rates. That specificity is what SPS attendees expect, and it is what distinguishes the fair from more conference-oriented events.

For 2026, industrial cybersecurity is likely to gain further floor space. The Cyber Resilience Act and NIS-2 are now binding compliance frameworks for manufacturers operating in or supplying into the European market, and compliance with new regulatory requirements was explicitly named as one of the key discussion drivers at SPS 2025. Exhibitors covering OT security, secure industrial communication protocols, and compliance tooling for connected production environments are positioning for increased buyer interest.

Software-defined automation is also emerging as a structural theme. The convergence of IT and OT disciplines, which the SPS "Automation meets IT" hall concept has been building toward for several years, reflects a broader industry direction: automation stacks that are increasingly hardware-agnostic and managed through software layers, closer in architecture to enterprise IT systems than to traditional PLC-based setups.

Who Attends and What They Are Evaluating

The highly-qualified visitors come to the exhibition with specific problems. During intensive discussions at the stands, they can compare solutions from different manufacturers to find the right solution for the automation tasks in their companies. The visitor mix covers machine tool builders, automotive production engineers, plant engineering firms, process machinery specialists, and electrical measurement and controls teams, among others.

Roughly two-thirds of visitors are involved in construction, management, or production roles, which means SPS skews heavily toward practitioners rather than researchers or policy observers. This matters for exhibitors: the conversations happening at booths are typically grounded in operational requirements, budget realities, and integration constraints rather than theoretical interest.

The exhibition saw a notable rise in international presence, with foreign exhibitors making up 44 percent of the total at SPS 2025. Countries including Italy, China, and the United States contributed significant exhibitor contingents, reflecting the fact that SPS is now a genuinely global procurement and technology-assessment event, not simply a German domestic showcase.

For buyers sourcing automation components and systems, the breadth of supply-side representation is a material advantage. Comparing sensor manufacturers from Germany, Taiwan, and Japan, or drive systems suppliers from Italy and the US, within a single three-day visit is difficult to replicate through any other format.

Exhibitor Structure and What It Reveals About the Supply Chain

SPS Nuremberg gathers leaders in the automation industry while also providing a platform for medium-sized and small companies. More than 30 percent of all exhibitors come from abroad, mainly from Europe and Asia. They specialize in electric drive systems and components, control, interface and sensor technology, human-machine interface, industrial communication, mechanical infrastructure, software and IT. BEA

That structure tells you something about how the automation supply chain is currently organized. The fair is not dominated by a handful of platform vendors. There is a thick layer of specialist mid-size companies serving defined niches in motion control, fieldbus components, edge computing hardware, and machine safety systems. For procurement teams, this means SPS is one of the few places where alternative suppliers for specific component categories can be evaluated side by side, reducing the sourcing risk that comes from over-reliance on a single integrator or platform ecosystem.

The Start-up Area and "Young Innovators" section, which drew considerable attention at SPS 2025, also reflects a supply-side trend: a new cohort of software-native automation companies entering an industry that has historically been dominated by hardware-first incumbents. Buyers evaluating next-generation architectures around software-defined control or AI-enabled process monitoring will find the startup zone increasingly relevant.

Practical Considerations for SPS 2026

SPS 2026 brings together over 1,000 exhibitors and an international audience from 24 to 26 November 2026 in Nuremberg, focusing on concrete solutions, digital trends and practical innovations. Exhibitor registration is already open, and the 15-hall layout at NürnbergMesse follows a topic-based allocation system, meaning buyers can plan their visit around specific technology categories rather than walking the entire floor without a clear route.

The fair is trade-only, which has a meaningful effect on the quality of floor conversations. Without general public attendance diluting the audience, exhibitor staff can engage immediately at a technical and commercial level. For international visitors, Nuremberg Airport sits approximately 12 kilometers from the exhibition center, and direct subway connections from the city center make the logistics straightforward.

In 2026, SPS will be happening for the 36th time. That institutional depth matters. The exhibitor advisory board structure, established in 1997, means the topic organization at SPS is shaped by actual industry practitioners rather than by organizer preferences alone. That is not a minor distinction for a fair covering a technical domain as specific as industrial automation.

Looking Ahead to November

The visitor growth trajectory from 2022 through 2025 suggests the European automation industry is treating SPS as more central to its commercial calendar, not less. The 2025 numbers, achieved despite adverse weather conditions and a difficult economic environment for German manufacturing, point to an event with structural relevance that goes beyond cyclical industry sentiment.

For SPS 2026, the question is whether the conversations that started around industrial AI deployment, cybersecurity compliance, and software-defined architectures at the 2025 edition will have matured into procurement decisions. The organizations attending in November will be measuring supplier readiness against those real implementation timelines, and exhibitors capable of demonstrating deployed solutions rather than roadmap promises are likely to command the most attention.
