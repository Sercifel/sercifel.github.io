# What the EU Machinery Regulation Means for Production Equipment Buyers Before 2027

![What the EU Machinery Regulation Means for Production Equipment Buyers Before 2027](https://i.meee.com.tw/p9tEnXE.png)

Regulation (EU) 2023/1230 on machinery will apply broadly from 20 January 2027, replacing the Machinery Directive 2006/42/EC. Although manufacturers carry most design and conformity obligations, production equipment buyers will feel the effects through specifications, supplier selection, import arrangements, acceptance testing, documentation, and later upgrades.

The change does not mean that factories must replace existing equipment. Products placed on the EU market in conformity with the current Directive before 20 January 2027 may continue to be made available. For projects crossing the deadline, however, the order date alone is not decisive. Buyers must establish when the equipment will be placed on the market or put into service and which company will act as manufacturer, importer, or integrator.

## Why the Legal Change Matters to Procurement

The existing framework is a directive implemented through national laws. The new framework is a regulation directly applicable across EU Member States. It also addresses digital instructions, connected machinery, software-related safety risks, certain machine-learning applications, and substantial physical or digital modifications.

Long-lead equipment ordered in 2026 may not be commissioned until after the new rules apply. A requirement such as “the machine must be CE marked” is therefore too vague. Procurement documents should identify the applicable legislation and require the supplier to explain the conformity route.

|Procurement question|What the buyer should confirm|
|---|---|
|Transition timing|When the machine will be placed on the market or put into service|
|Economic operator|Who is the manufacturer, EU importer, or distributor|
|Conformity route|Whether internal control is permitted or a notified body is required|
|Handover package|Which declarations, instructions, records, and test reports will be supplied|

## Buyers Can Acquire Legal Responsibilities

A company buying a standard machine from an EU supplier will normally remain an end user. Its position can change when it imports machinery directly from outside the EU, sells it under its own brand, manufactures machinery for its own use, integrates partly completed machinery, or makes a substantial modification.

An EU importer must verify that the manufacturer completed the appropriate conformity assessment and supplied the required declaration, instructions, identification, and traceability information. The importer must retain the EU declaration of conformity for at least ten years and help authorities obtain technical documentation on request.

For direct overseas sourcing, a CE mark does not remove the need to identify who holds EU-side obligations. Contracts should name the responsible economic operator, the location of compliance records, and the process for corrective action.

## Cybersecurity Is Now Part of Machinery Safety

The Regulation adds requirements for hardware, software, and data whose corruption could affect machinery safety. Safety-relevant systems must be protected against accidental or intentional interference. Machinery must also identify software needed for safe operation and retain evidence of certain interventions or configuration changes.

The focus is not general cybersecurity but digital interference that could affect safety. Buyers of connected equipment should still ask how remote access, user permissions, updates, backups, safety PLC changes, and version control are managed. Specifications should define who may install updates, how they are validated, what records will be delivered, and how long safety-relevant support will remain available.

## AI-Related Safety Requires Careful Classification

The Regulation gives special treatment to machinery and safety components using fully or partially self-evolving behaviour based on machine learning when that behaviour ensures a safety function. These categories appear in Annex I, Part A and require conformity assessment involving a notified body.

Most automated machines will not fall into this category merely because they use sensors or programmed logic. Buyers should ask whether machine learning changes system behaviour and whether that behaviour influences a safety function. Where it does, the supplier should identify the classification, assessment procedure, notified body, safety limits, and supporting records.

## Digital Instructions Need Durable Access

Manufacturers may provide instructions digitally if access is clearly indicated and users can download, print, and store the files. Instructions must remain accessible online for the expected machine lifetime and for at least ten years after market placement. A buyer may request paper instructions free of charge at purchase.

A temporary link is not a complete handover package. Buyers should require durable formats, revision numbers, local-language instructions, maintenance procedures, diagrams, backup copies, and a process for receiving updated safety information.

## Modifications May Turn the Buyer Into the Manufacturer

A substantial modification may be physical or digital. It can arise when an unplanned alteration made after market placement or commissioning creates a new hazard or increases an existing risk and requires specified changes to guarding, safety controls, stability, or mechanical strength.

The company carrying out such a modification is treated as the manufacturer for the affected machinery. This may affect projects involving higher speeds, robot integration, altered guards, revised safety logic, or several machines combined into a line.

Not every upgrade is substantial. The conclusion should follow a documented risk assessment. Factories should therefore use formal change control involving engineering, safety, maintenance, and operational technology personnel, with supplier input where appropriate.

## Practical Actions Before 2027

Compliance questions should enter the tender rather than waiting for final acceptance. Specifications should state the destination country, intended use, expected commissioning date, integration boundaries, required languages, and planned software or production changes.

Supplier reviews should confirm the Annex I classification, applicable standards, conformity procedure, notified-body involvement, importer identity, and document-retention arrangements. Contracts should allocate responsibility for integration, safety validation, software updates, training, technical records, and corrective actions.

Acceptance tests should cover not only production output but also guards, interlocks, emergency stops, safety functions, alarms, foreseeable misuse, access permissions, software versions, and document completeness.

## FAQ

### Does existing machinery need to be recertified in 2027?

No. Equipment lawfully placed on the market under the Directive before 20 January 2027 is not automatically subject to recertification.

### Is an order placed before 2027 governed by the old Directive?

Not necessarily. Market-placement or commissioning circumstances matter more than the purchase-order date.

### Will every machine require a notified body?

No. The procedure depends on the category. Annex I, Part A machinery requires third-party assessment, while other machinery may permit internal production control.

### Can suppliers provide only digital manuals?

Yes, when the Regulation’s access, download, storage, and availability conditions are met. Paper copies can be requested free of charge at purchase.

### Does adding a robot automatically count as a substantial modification?

No. The integration must be assessed for new hazards, increased risks, and required protective measures.

### Are the cybersecurity provisions separate from safety compliance?

They address digital interference where it can affect essential health and safety requirements. Other EU cybersecurity rules may also apply.

## Conclusion

The 2027 transition affects delivery timing, importer duties, conformity assessment, software integrity, AI-enabled safety functions, digital documentation, and responsibility for modifications.

Buyers can reduce commissioning delays and disputes by defining these issues before awarding a contract. The goal is to make each party’s technical, commercial, and legal responsibilities clear before the machine enters the factory.
