# Reducing Customer Rejection Rates: The Role of Machine Stability in Blown Film Production
![](https://www.matilataiwan.com/storage/media/products/cate/blown-film-machine/list/Multi-Layer-Blown%20-Film-Machine/CT-ABA.jpg)

In the plastic packaging industry, the way we make film is changing. For many years, a single-layer (mono-layer) machine was the standard. It was simple to operate and required less initial investment. However, the market now demands thinner, stronger, and clearer films. This is where 3-layer co-extrusion machinery, such as the Matila CT-ML series, becomes a necessary tool for growth.

## The Limitations of Mono-Layer Production

A mono-layer machine uses one screw and one barrel to melt plastic resin. This resin is then blown into a single-layer bubble. Because there is only one layer, every ingredient in the plastic mix is present throughout the entire thickness of the film.

If a manufacturer wants a film to be strong, they must add specific resins. If they want it to be clear, they add others. However, when these resins are mixed in a single layer, they often work against each other. For example, adding stiffness often makes the film more brittle or less clear. To compensate for these weaknesses, manufacturers usually make the film thicker. Thick film uses more raw material, which increases the cost of every roll produced.

## How 3-Layer (ABC) Technology Works

A 3-layer co-extrusion machine uses three separate screws and barrels. These three streams of molten plastic meet in a specialized die head. They are layered together—like a sandwich—before being blown into a bubble. This is commonly called an ABC structure.

1. **The Outer Layer (A):** This layer provides the visual and surface properties. Manufacturers can use high-quality resins like Metallocene LLDPE here to ensure the film is shiny and clear.
    
2. **The Middle Layer (B):** This is the "skeleton" of the film. It often uses cheaper, stiffer resins like HDPE. Because it is hidden in the middle, it provides strength without making the surface of the film look dull.
    
3. **The Inner Layer (C):** This layer can be designed for specific needs, such as "high-slip" properties so the film moves easily through packaging machines or "sealant" properties for bags.
    

## The Data Behind the Upgrade

When we look at real-world production data, the difference between these two methods is measurable. In a recent study of a packaging plant, a 60-micron mono-layer film was compared to a 45-micron 3-layer film.

The 3-layer film was 25% thinner, yet its tensile strength (the force needed to pull it apart) was 27% higher. This is because the 3-layer process allows for the precise placement of strengthening resins. Additionally, the "haze" measurement—which tracks how cloudy the film looks—dropped from 12% to 6%. A lower haze percentage means the film is twice as clear.

## Financial Benefits for Distributors and Agents

For a distributor, selling 3-layer machinery is not just about selling a better tool; it is about selling a better business model.

- **Material Savings:** If a factory can reduce film thickness from 60 microns to 45 microns, they save 25% on resin costs. In a high-volume factory, this can save tens of thousands of dollars every month.
    
- **Higher Output:** Modern 3-layer lines often have higher output rates. For example, moving from 120 kg/hr to 180 kg/hr represents a 50% increase in production capacity without needing more floor space.
    
- **Reduced Rejection:** High-quality 3-layer machines use advanced cooling systems, such as dual-lip air rings. These rings cool the plastic bubble quickly and evenly. This prevents the plastic from forming large crystals, which keeps the film clear and consistent. This leads to a much lower rejection rate from end customers.
    

## Key Machine Features to Look For

When evaluating a manufacturer like Matila, distributors should focus on three technical areas:

**1. Precision Air Rings** Cooling is the most important part of making clear film. If the film stays hot for too long, it becomes "hazy." A dual-lip air ring provides a stable flow of air that "freezes" the plastic structure at the exact right moment. This ensures the film remains as clear as glass.

**2. Specialized Screw Design** Different resins melt at different temperatures. A good 3-layer machine uses screws designed to mix resins thoroughly without causing "thermal degradation" (overheating). Overheated plastic creates yellow spots or "gels" in the film, which leads to customer complaints.

**3. Bubble Stability** At high speeds, the plastic bubble can start to vibrate or "dance." If the bubble is not stable, the thickness of the film will vary. This is called "gauge variation." High-end machines use stable frames and precise air control to keep the bubble perfectly still, even at high production speeds.

## Why This Matters for the Future

Global brands are moving toward "Sustainable Packaging." This usually means using less plastic to do the same job. This process is called **Downgauging**. A company that only owns mono-layer machines cannot participate in this trend. They are forced to produce heavy, expensive, and cloudy films. By moving to 3-layer technology, manufacturers can meet sustainability goals while also increasing their own profits.
