# ITM Industry Europe 2026: Why Poznań Still Matters for Central Europe’s Manufacturing Supply Chain
![](https://www.autogrip-machinery.com/web/image/23216-e6766ab6/itm2026_1920x1080_en.jpg?access_token=bf182d40-4250-4ffa-b756-391c9b95f39b)

ITM Industry Europe 2026 is scheduled for 26 to 29 May 2026 at the Poznań International Fair grounds, with Grupa MTP again positioning the event as a broad industrial meeting point rather than a narrow machinery showcase. The fair will run alongside Modernlog and the Subcontracting Industrial Cooperation Fair, which is central to its appeal. It allows visitors to look at production equipment, automation, logistics systems and supplier cooperation within one trade fair block rather than across separate events.

That format matters because industrial purchasing has become more layered. A factory upgrade is rarely about one machine alone. Buyers are comparing machine tools, automation compatibility, process control, handling systems, subcontracting options and implementation risk at the same time. ITM’s structure reflects that reality better than a single-segment show. Official event materials for 2026 continue to highlight machine tools, automation and robotics, welding, surface treatment and related production technologies as core pillars of the exhibition.

## Poznań’s position is part of the event’s commercial logic

Poznań remains a practical host city for this kind of exhibition because it sits inside one of Europe’s most important regional manufacturing belts. Poland’s industrial base connects closely with German and wider European supply chains in sectors such as machinery, automotive components, fabricated metal products and engineering subcontracting. That gives the fair relevance beyond the domestic market. For exhibitors, ITM is a route into Central and Eastern European demand. For buyers, it is a regional sourcing and benchmarking stop that is easier to navigate than some of the continent’s larger, more diffuse industrial fairs.

The wider market context also supports a more practical reading of the event in 2026. Eurostat’s latest industrial production release showed only modest movement across the EU, pointing to a recovery that remains uneven rather than broad-based. Poland’s March 2026 manufacturing PMI improved to 48.7 from 47.1 in February, with output returning to growth, but the index still remained below the neutral 50 level. In other words, factories are still spending, but they are likely to spend carefully. That tends to favour trade fairs where buyers can make direct technical comparisons and discuss implementation details with suppliers face to face.

## The latest edition suggests solid regional traction, not just symbolic scale

The 2025 edition offers a useful indicator of where the event stands. Official ITM 2026 materials report 738 exhibitors, 14,673 visitors, 24 exhibitor countries and seven conference stages for the latest completed edition. Those numbers do not place ITM in the same bracket as Europe’s very largest industrial expos, but they are strong enough to confirm that Poznań remains a meaningful market platform for companies targeting industrial buyers in Poland and neighbouring markets.

The visitor profile matters as much as the headline totals. According to official event materials, the audience includes directors, plant managers, technologists, engineers, specialists, operators and technicians, with strong representation from metalworking, machinery engineering, automotive, transport, aviation, construction and electronics. That mix suggests a fair where conversations are likely to move beyond first-contact networking into more detailed discussions around specification, process fit and purchasing intent. For suppliers, that is usually a more valuable signal than raw footfall alone.

## The strongest value often sits below the headline equipment

Large machines and robotic cells tend to dominate the visual identity of industrial exhibitions, but the practical value of a show like ITM often sits one level below that. Many of the most commercially relevant discussions happen around tooling, workholding, control systems, sensors, pneumatics, software, metrology, welding processes and surface engineering. These are the categories that shape machine utilisation, setup efficiency, repeatability, maintenance demands and product quality once equipment reaches the factory floor.

That is one reason exhibitor structure matters. A fair becomes more useful when buyers can assess both the main investment and the supporting technologies that determine whether it will perform as expected in production. In the case of ITM 2026, that includes specialist suppliers in areas such as clamping and workholding. One example is [AUTOGRIP Machinery](https://www.autogrip-machinery.com/blog/exhibition-4/itm-2026-101), which has announced its participation in the show and publicly identifies its focus on power chucks, rotary cylinders, collet chucks and related clamping products. In the context of ITM, that is relevant less as a standalone brand story and more as a reminder that machining productivity is often shaped by subsystem choices as much as by the machine tool itself.

## 2026 buyers are likely to arrive with tighter project logic

The mood going into ITM 2026 looks more operational than expansive. Manufacturers are still balancing labour pressure, energy costs, productivity targets and uneven downstream demand. In that environment, visitors usually come to fairs with a narrower set of questions and a clearer idea of where investment must pay off. They want to know whether a machine can reduce setup time, whether a robot cell will fit an existing process, whether software can improve traceability without excessive integration work and whether a supplier can support commissioning and service after the event. The latest PMI data, with output improving but the overall index still below 50, points to exactly this kind of cautious but active procurement environment.

This should favour exhibitors that can discuss applications in specific terms rather than rely on broad claims about innovation. ITM has traditionally benefited from the presence of working machinery and demonstration-led presentations and that remains one of its strengths. Buyers in metalworking and automation typically learn more from watching equipment run, seeing changeover logic, or discussing part-specific process issues than from a polished brand message. That practical emphasis is part of what keeps the fair relevant year after year.

## A broader fair block makes more sense in a phased investment cycle

The co-location with Modernlog and Subcontracting is not just convenient programming. It reflects how many industrial projects now unfold. A manufacturer may start by looking at machining, welding, or automation, then realise that the project also needs internal transport changes, warehouse support, production outsourcing, or a different supplier base for components and assemblies. By linking these areas in one visit, Poznań gives buyers a more realistic way to evaluate next steps.

This matters especially in a period when capital spending often happens in stages rather than as a single large commitment. Companies may approve a machining upgrade, a welding cell, or a process improvement first, then build around it with logistics changes or supplier partnerships later. ITM’s structure fits that incremental approach well. It is one of the reasons the event works as a business fair rather than just an industry showcase. Visitors can move from machine-side decisions to adjacent operational issues without losing context.

## Robotics and digitalisation will remain visible, but buyers will judge them by execution

ITM’s 2026 messaging continues to lean heavily into automation, robotics, process improvement and factory modernisation. That is consistent with wider market signals. The International Federation of Robotics reported 542,000 industrial robot installations worldwide in 2024, marking the fourth consecutive year above half a million units, while Europe accounted for 16 percent of new deployments. Those figures help explain why robotics, cobots, data use and factory integration will remain central discussion points in Poznań.

Still, the interesting question at ITM is not whether digitalisation matters. It is where buyers think it can be applied first with acceptable cost and manageable risk. For some factories, that may mean robot-assisted handling. For others, it may mean process data, safety systems, welding control, inspection, or a more efficient machine-side setup. The fair’s value lies in allowing these priorities to be compared across applications rather than discussed only at the level of trend language. That makes ITM more useful for decision-makers who are under pressure to justify each step of a project.

## Why ITM Industry Europe 2026 is still worth watching

ITM Industry Europe 2026 matters because it occupies a commercially useful middle ground. It is large enough to attract serious industrial traffic, focused enough to stay practical and broad enough to reflect how manufacturing investments are actually made in Central Europe. The fair is not simply about showcasing technology. It is about giving buyers a place to compare machinery, subsystems, automation, logistics and supplier capacity in one regional setting.

For exhibitors, that means access to a buyer base that is increasingly selective and technically engaged. For visitors, it means a chance to evaluate not only flagship equipment, but also the surrounding technologies and supplier relationships that determine whether production projects succeed after the show. That is why Poznań continues to hold its place on the industrial trade fair calendar. The event remains useful not because it promises everything, but because it aligns closely with how factories actually buy, upgrade and source.
