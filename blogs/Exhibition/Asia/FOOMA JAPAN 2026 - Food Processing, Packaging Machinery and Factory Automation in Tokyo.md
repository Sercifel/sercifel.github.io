# FOOMA JAPAN 2026: Food Processing, Packaging Machinery and Factory Automation in Tokyo
![](https://i.meee.com.tw/jvADDDW.jpg)

FOOMA JAPAN 2026 will take place from June 2 to 5, 2026 at Tokyo Big Sight, using West Hall 1 to 4 and East Hall 1 to 3, 7 and 8. Organized by the Japan Food Machinery Manufacturers’ Association, the exhibition returns under the theme “The Shift is On.” That slogan is broad, but in the context of Japan’s food manufacturing sector, it points to a clear set of pressures: labor shortages, rising expectations around food safety, the need for better productivity, and a steady push toward more automated plant operations.

What keeps FOOMA relevant is that it is not designed as a finished food or retail-focused event. It is a production technology exhibition built around the machinery and systems that keep food factories moving. That includes processing, preparation, packaging, inspection, hygiene control, logistics, plant engineering, and digital tools tied to manufacturing performance. For buyers, this makes FOOMA less about consumer-facing product trends and more about how food production can be improved at factory level.

## Why FOOMA still carries weight in the Asian food manufacturing calendar

FOOMA has been running since 1978, and its longevity matters because it reflects how deeply embedded the show is in Japan’s manufacturing base. According to the official organizer, the exhibition is positioned as a comprehensive showcase covering food manufacturing from upstream to downstream. That gives it a different role from narrower packaging-only or ingredient-led trade fairs.

Its scale also supports that standing. Official FOOMA figures show that FOOMA JAPAN 2025 drew 1,007 exhibitors and 110,827 visitors, with more than 5,000 solutions presented across the venue. JETRO’s fair listing adds that the previous edition included 4,569 foreign visitors and 14 foreign exhibitors, while the planned 2026 floor size is listed at 35,262 square meters. Those numbers suggest a show with a strong domestic core, but one that is also attracting broader regional interest from overseas buyers, distributors, and suppliers looking at Japan’s food machinery sector more seriously.

## Tokyo’s role is part of the exhibition’s commercial value

FOOMA’s location is not incidental. Tokyo remains Japan’s business center, and for many international exhibitors it is the easiest entry point into the country’s food manufacturing market. More importantly, Japan is still seen as a demanding environment for food production equipment. Buyers in this market tend to place high importance on consistency, hygiene, reliability, space efficiency, and handling precision.

That is why FOOMA often functions as more than a domestic exhibition. It is also a reference point for what high-spec food production looks like in practice. For regional buyers from Asia, visiting FOOMA can be a way to compare Japanese machinery strengths across multiple stages of the manufacturing process, especially in categories where uptime, cleanliness, and process control matter more than headline novelty.

## The buyer agenda in 2026 will likely be shaped by labor pressure

The strongest reason to watch FOOMA JAPAN 2026 is the operating reality inside food plants. Japan’s Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries has noted that food manufacturing remains labor intensive, with roughly 1.3 million people employed in the sector, and that policy direction is increasingly tied to improving productivity through automation. Broader manufacturing sentiment also supports that reading. Cabinet Office reporting based on the BOJ Tankan showed that manufacturers were still facing labor shortage pressure in late 2024.

That background matters because it changes how visitors approach the show floor. Automation at FOOMA is not a speculative topic. It is tied to staffing constraints, production continuity, sanitation demands, and the need to reduce reliance on manual tasks that are difficult to maintain at scale. In practical terms, buyers are likely to be looking for robotics, automated transfer systems, inspection technology, integrated control systems, and line upgrades that reduce labor dependency without compromising output quality.

## A show floor built around plant-wide sourcing, not isolated categories

One of FOOMA’s main strengths is its breadth. The exhibitor structure from the 2025 edition shows how wide the sourcing scope really is. Categories ranged from food materials processing and preparation machinery to systems for bakery, confectionery, meat, seafood, noodle products, deli foods, beverages, dairy, tofu, and fermented foods. They also included engineering services, robots, IT and IoT, quality maintenance, packaging and filling, storage and logistics, inspection systems, hygiene solutions, environmental protection, recycling, and machinery components.

This matters because investment decisions in food manufacturing are rarely limited to one machine. A processor sourcing a packaging upgrade may also be reviewing inspection accuracy, sanitation design, conveyor handling, traceability tools, or end-of-line efficiency. FOOMA works well because it brings those interdependent decisions into a single venue. For engineers, procurement teams, plant managers, and technical directors, that makes the exhibition more useful than events where processing, packaging, and factory systems are split across separate shows.

## What the exhibitor mix says about Japan’s food production priorities

FOOMA’s exhibitor profile also offers a useful read on how food manufacturing investment is organized in Japan. This is not just a venue for complete line builders or major OEMs. It is also a place where buyers meet sensor providers, inspection specialists, sanitation equipment suppliers, software companies, logistics system firms, and engineering consultants.

That mix says something important about the market. Food machinery buying in Japan is often tied closely to specification, validation, hygiene design, and long-term operational performance. In other words, purchasing decisions are not purely commercial. They often sit between engineering, compliance, food safety, maintenance, and total line efficiency. For overseas suppliers trying to enter the market, that means FOOMA is useful not only for lead generation, but also for understanding how buyer priorities are structured and where decision-making authority tends to sit.

## Waste reduction and hygienic design are becoming more commercial issues

Sustainability will also remain an important theme at FOOMA JAPAN 2026, but not in a superficial sense. Japan’s policy direction on food loss and waste reduction is becoming more concrete, with official targets to cut business food loss and waste significantly by fiscal 2030 compared with fiscal 2000 levels. In food factories, those targets translate into practical technology demand rather than broad messaging.

That is likely to support interest in equipment that improves yield, reduces product giveaway, lowers false rejects, uses packaging materials more efficiently, and handles by-products more effectively. At the same time, hygienic design remains closely tied to food safety, labor efficiency, and cleaning performance. When FOOMA highlights safety and hygienic design information, including EHEDG-related content, it reflects a market where sanitation design is not a side topic. It is increasingly part of the commercial logic behind equipment replacement and plant upgrades.

## FOOMA is also a signal platform for where the industry is heading

Another reason the event matters is that FOOMA is not only about physical equipment displays. The 2026 visitor program includes seminars and symposiums, an Academic Plaza that connects industry, government, and academia, and a FOOMA Award framework that highlights machinery contributing to productivity, labor shortage response, and new food development.

That knowledge layer gives the exhibition extra value. Buyers are not simply comparing specifications. They are also tracking where Japanese machinery makers see future demand, which technologies are being framed as commercially relevant, and how standards around hygiene, automation, and smart manufacturing are developing. For suppliers, the show can be read as a market signal platform as much as a sales venue.

## Why 2026 should be watched as a commercially serious edition

The latest official numbers suggest FOOMA enters 2026 from a position of strength. Attendance in 2025 was slightly below the previous year, but it still remained above 110,000 visitors, while exhibitor participation reached a record 1,007 companies. That balance is worth noting. It suggests that supplier confidence stayed strong, and that the event continues to attract serious investment from machinery makers even if visitor volumes fluctuate modestly year to year.

For buyers, that usually points to a healthier trade fair environment. High exhibitor density often means better comparison opportunities, more complete sourcing coverage, and a stronger chance of finding solutions that match actual plant investment needs rather than promotional talking points.

## For buyers and suppliers, FOOMA JAPAN 2026 is about fit, not just visibility

For international suppliers, FOOMA JAPAN 2026 should not be approached as a generic branding opportunity. Japan’s market expects clear value in reliability, hygiene, footprint, production consistency, and support capability. The exhibition is most useful for companies that can show where they fit into those requirements.

For buyers across Asia, the show offers something slightly different. It provides a concentrated view of how Japanese food manufacturing technology is evolving across processing, packaging, automation, inspection, sanitation, and plant integration. That makes it a useful checkpoint for companies comparing equipment philosophies, benchmarking plant upgrades, or identifying technology partners for long-term production improvement.

Visitor pre-registration is scheduled to open on April 1, 2026. For companies planning major line investments, supplier reviews, or market entry discussions, FOOMA JAPAN 2026 looks set to remain one of the most commercially relevant food machinery exhibitions in the region.
